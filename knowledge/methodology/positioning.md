# Brand Positioning

> Positioning is the decision about where a brand stands in the mind of a specific customer, against a specific alternative, in a specific moment. ARTO treats it as an operating decision, codified in the system, not a tagline exercise.

## The ARTO take

Most agencies start positioning from the brand inward: vision, mission, values, personality. ARTO starts from the customer and the category. A positioning is only real if a named customer can repeat it back and use it to choose between the brand and the next option on their list.

ARTO operates as a system, not an org chart. Strategic knowledge lives in the system, not in people. Positioning work produces two artifacts at once: a Brief that the founder can defend in a room, and a set of rules codified as skills that every downstream workflow can access. Every project refines the system. (Source: ARTO Signal 007, April 2026.)

Three non-negotiables:

1. Positioning is a choice, not a summary. It names what the brand is for and what it is not. The NOT is as important as the IS.
2. Positioning is written against a competitor, never in a vacuum. If the statement survives when you swap in the competitor's name, it is not positioning. Benchmarking is mandatory and named, not gestural.
3. Positioning has to carry the weight of a decision. The founder should be able to use it to approve or kill a product, a channel, a hire, or a price.

Positioning work sits inside ARTO's Creative and Design Strategy service line, feeds the IRL line when activations are part of the rollout, and is priced in credit packages (Plan A 5 hours through Plan F 60 hours) when the client wants to flex scope. Bilingual delivery is part of the craft. ARTO writes ES and EN in parallel.

Internal operating principle: ARTO treats contractors as team, not as vendors. That changes how positioning gets communicated internally. The Brief and the Brand Essence are written so any teammate, whether in-house or extended, can pick them up cold and act on them. The methodology is brand-agnostic by design. It lives in the system so it survives any single client.

## The process

Five phases. 4 to 10 weeks depending on scope. The Lavandería engagement in March 2026 ran a 4-week branding scope for MXN $40,000; HandLab and Seevra style engagements run 8 to 10 weeks.

Phase 1: Intake, qualification, kickoff (week 1)

- Prospect fills the ARTO Branding Questionnaire: six blocks covering brand identity and story, target audience and market position, messaging, visual identity, marketing, operational insights, plus bonus contrast axes (masculine to feminine, modern to classic, steady to high-speed).
- Discovery call runs the ARTO 4-filter qualification: urgency, decision maker, pricing fit, commitment. No positioning work starts until all 4 clear.
- Pipeline is codified: Pipedrive lead, deal folder with credentials, Wrike task opens automatically, Notion client hub linked to Client Database, Proveedor assigned from Proveedores Hubs.
- Deliverable: signed proposal or SOW, kickoff date, stakeholder map, first-draft Brief skeleton.

Phase 2: The Brief (week 1 to 2)

The Brief is the first authored artifact and the one the rest of the project answers back to. Structure before copy, per the ARTO operating rule: define the shape first, fill it second. Standard ARTO Brief, 11 sections (template used on the Lavandería engagement in March 2026 and reused as the working pattern):

1. Contexto del proyecto
2. Objetivo del proyecto
3. Concepto estratégico (the guiding metaphor, written as "the X of Y," for example "the IKEA of laundromats")
4. Target (named, segmented, with the insight clave that kills lazy positioning)
5. Investigación de mercado (3 to 5 named competitors with hallazgos clave per competitor)
6. Posicionamiento (one sentence, under 30 words, says what the brand is and is not)
7. Dirección creativa
8. Experiencia en tienda o producto (category-dependent)
9. Experiencia de usuario
10. Comunicación de marca (tono in 3 words, applications)
11. Diferenciadores clave

The Brief is signed off by the founder before visual work starts.

Phase 3: Brand Essence Document (week 2 to 3)

Written artifact, not a deck. 9 sections: (1) vision and mission, (2) problem solved, (3) positioning table (categoría, acción central, entregable, diferencial, mercado inicial), (4) value proposition with 3 pillars and a tagline, (5) primary user and secondary users with buy trigger, (6) personality and voice with an así sí / así no table, (7) differentiators, (8) tagline and key messages by audience segment, (9) what the brand is NOT.

Positioning statement spine: [Brand] is the [category] that [central action] for [audience] who need [outcome], without [the alternative's failure mode].

Reference exemplars in the knowledge base: Seevra Brand Essence v1.0 (Feb 2026, polished form), Noro Brand Essence v2.0 (2025, working form). The Brand Essence is versioned; it is not a one-shot.

Phase 4: Identity system, messaging, application (week 3 to 8)

- Naming, Visual Paths exploration (up to 2 creative routes), Stylesheet.
- Core deliverables per the ARTO Brand Assets taxonomy: Logo / Wordmark, Paleta de color (Color Battery), Jerarquía tipográfica, Elementos gráficos, Stylesheet (manual base), Characters when the brand calls for them.
- 3 collateral pieces as applied proof: redes sociales, posters, papelería, or category-specific pieces (señalética, uniformes, empaque).
- Messaging architecture in ES and EN by audience segment.
- Brand assets land in the Notion Brand Assets database tagged by Asset Type (Font, Logo, Image, Social, Brandbook, Characters, Manual, Comunicación Corp., FIGMA) and File Type.

Phase 5: Handover, codification, 30-day check (week 8 onward)

- 1-hour handover training. Client team learns to use the positioning as a decision tool, not a reference document.
- The positioning gets codified into the system. In active ARTO engagements this means skills installed in the client's workspace that carry the positioning forward. The Noro editorial system (March 2026) is the reference case: 8 Writer Agents trained on brand essence pillars, RAG knowledge base owned by client, 30-criteria Quality Assurance on every output. The brand no longer lives in a PDF; it lives in a running system.
- 30-day review: look at the first 3 decisions the client made using the positioning. If the positioning did not help decide, the positioning is wrong. Revise.
- Deliverable: post-engagement report, open items, upsell paths (web, ecommerce, campaign, IRL activation, agent codification).

Operating rules for the team (aligned to ARTO's internal operating principles, the 10 mandamientos de Notion):

- Structure before copy. Define the Brief shape first, fill it second. The 11 sections are the shape.
- Every engagement produces a Brief and a Brand Essence. These are the two artifacts that survive and that any teammate, in-house or extended, can pick up cold.
- Positioning work is written for the team, which includes contractors. Language, references, and rationale must be clear enough that a teammate who joined yesterday can act on it.
- Write positioning in sentences, not bullets. If it cannot survive as a paragraph, it is not finished.
- Every positioning ships with its NOT table and its metaphor ("the X of Y"). No exceptions. If there is no metaphor, the positioning is not ready.
- Competitors go on the wall from day 1, named, minimum 3. Benchmarking includes at least one brand outside the category the client admires.
- If the client cannot repeat the positioning back in their own words by end of week 3, stop and rewrite.
- Deliver in ES and EN when the client operates across both. Write in parallel, do not translate at the end.
- Everything lives in the system. If it is not in Notion (Brand Essence, Brief, Brand Assets, Client Hub), it does not exist.
- Document the "cómo" and the "por qué," not only the what. Six months later, a new operator has to read it cold and understand why the call was made.
- The positioning must leave ARTO as a codified asset (document plus skill or prompt set), not only as a PDF.

## Anti-patterns

Signals a positioning is mediocre or broken.

- Starts with "We are a [adjective] [adjective] [adjective] brand that..." Three adjectives in a row means the team could not commit to one.
- Works equally well for a competitor. Swap in the competitor's name, if the sentence still reads, it is category boilerplate.
- Talks about the brand's feelings (passionate, bold, innovative) instead of the customer's outcome.
- Uses the words leverage, empower, revolutionize, seamless, robust, holistic, cutting-edge, next-generation, ecosystem, disruptive, synergy, or "AI-powered." The full ARTO banned list.
- Mentions technology or process before customer. "AI-powered platform that..." is a feature, not a position.
- Cannot be used to say no. If the positioning does not rule out a product, a channel, or a customer type, it is a mission statement in costume.
- No metaphor ("the X of Y"). Good positioning has a metaphor the founder can repeat.
- No Brief behind it. No named competitors. No Ideal Customer Profile.
- Cannot be repeated by the founder without reading the slide.
- Breaks in translation. Works in English, falls apart in Spanish, or the reverse.
- Longer than 40 words.
- No NOT table. Every real positioning has a list of things the brand deliberately is not.
- No "Refs SÍ / Refs NO" pair. Every real positioning gives the team named references to hit and named references to avoid.
- Needs the strategist in the room to make sense. If a teammate who joined yesterday cannot read the Brief and act on it, the document is not operational.
- Lives only in a PDF. If six months later there is no way to query it from the workflow that actually produces content, the positioning will drift.

## What good looks like

Scored 1 to 10 against four criteria: specificity (names real customer, alternative, outcome), operability (can approve or kill a decision), portability (survives ES and EN, in short form, said out loud), and codification (can be queried by a downstream workflow or agent).

- Score 9, Seevra (Feb 2026). "The financial copilot that turns files into decisions. For professionals and teams who need models, projections, and reports ready to operate, without waiting on anyone." Names category, central action, audience, outcome, alternative failure mode. NOT table kills chat-only tools, ERPs, accounting software, educational assistants. Codified through tagline plus pillar structure. Founder can reject any feature that does not produce an editable document.
- Score 9, NOWFUL (Mar 2025). Repositioned from NoGender skincare to holistic wellness and self-care brand with inclusive, emotionally resonant identity, subscription plus a-la-carte monetization. Specific audience shift, category shift, monetization shift. Ruled out the old SKU mix and the Amazon-only channel dependency.
- Score 8, Noro (2025 to 2026). Regional media for the northwest of Mexico (BC, BCS, Sonora, Sinaloa, Nayarit, Chihuahua), contemporary voice, solutions-oriented narrative, built as a narrative collective. Now fully codified into 8 Writer Agents with distinct styles, RAG knowledge base, and 30-criterion QA. Loses a point because Brand Essence v2.0 still reads as post-its more than finished prose. First ARTO engagement where the positioning lives as a running system, not a document.
- Score 8, Lavandería (Mar 2026). "Una lavandería moderna, accesible y bien diseñada que prioriza la funcionalidad sin caer en lo genérico ni en lo aspiracional excesivo." Anchored by the metaphor "El IKEA de las lavanderías." Benchmarked against 6 named Madrid competitors (La Wash, Speed Queen, Open Wash, La Casa de la Colada, Lila Vanda, Soul) with specific hallazgos per competitor. Kills aspirational-cafe model from the start. Target insight clear: "el usuario no busca lujo, busca resolver rápido, fácil y a buen precio, pero en un espacio que no se sienta descuidado."
- Score 8, COMNOR (2018, Sigma Alimentos). Brand strategy based on segmentation by experience level in the kitchen, prioritizing simplicity and trust, designed for immediate legibility at shelf. Durable: still referenced in the portfolio 8 years later.
- Score 5, generic. "A modern brand for conscious consumers who care about quality." No category, no competitor, "conscious" and "quality" mean nothing operationally.
- Score 3, generic. "We empower visionary leaders to unlock their full potential with innovative, human-centered solutions." Every banned word, no customer, no alternative, no outcome.

## Recent shifts

What changed in the last 12 months that affects how ARTO approaches positioning, dated 2026-04-15.

- The agency deliverable moved from deck to system. ARTO now ships positioning as a document plus a codified set of skills, prompts, and agents that the client's workflow can query. "Ya no entregas un deck de estrategia. Entregas un sistema que opera." (ARTO Signal 007.) Implication: every engagement has a codification deliverable, not only a PDF.
- AI feature parity collapsed category differentiation. When every competitor ships an AI-powered version the same quarter, positioning has to move off the feature and onto the customer job. Implication: strip AI and tech language from the first draft, add back only what the customer would say themselves.
- The first-draft layer is compressed. Founders use GPT-class tools to write their own positioning before hiring ARTO. Implication: ARTO's value is the audit, the competitor confrontation, the Brief discipline, and the codification into a running system. The Brand Essence has to be sharper than a founder can self-generate in a weekend.
- Bilingual delivery is table stakes for LATAM-US brands. Clients expect ES and EN from day 1. Implication: write in parallel, treat tone drift across languages as a blocking bug.
- Founder-led short video is where positioning gets tested. If the founder cannot say the line on camera in 15 seconds, it will not survive distribution. Implication: every positioning is tested out loud by the founder before it ships.
- Private equity and search funds are buying mid-market brands at flat multiples. Positioning is now a valuation input. HandLab (Mar 2026) made this explicit. Implication: for founders who may sell within 24 months, frame positioning as an asset that raises the comp and write it so a buyer can read it.
- Regional and local identity is outperforming global polish in LATAM. Noro and the northwest-Mexico focus ran faster than pan-Latin attempts. Implication: do not sand down regional specificity. Lean into it.
- Client teams are leaner and AI-assisted. The Noro engagement shows the pattern: 3 juniors replaced by agents, 5 seniors operating the system, content still shipped at volume. Implication: positioning has to be directly usable by a senior operator with agents as their team, not by a 10-person marketing department.
- "Todo suena igual" is the category risk. Nayeli from Cultura Colectiva Colombia surfaced it in diligence: AI-generated content is homogenizing voices across media. Implication: ARTO differentiates positioning by building the client's distinctive knowledge base (RAG, tribal knowledge, QA criteria) into the system from week 1, not as an afterthought.
- The tooling stack shifts every quarter. Current ARTO stack as of Mar 2026: Claude Opus 4.6 for mega-context, Claude Sonnet 4.6 for copywriting, GPT 5.4 Pro for reasoning, Gemini 3 Pro for research, Nano Banana 2 for realistic image, Higgsfield for artistic image, Kling 3 for video, ElevenLabs for voice, Lovable for apps, Gamma for decks. Implication: the methodology is tool-agnostic, the tool stack is versioned. Review every 90 days.
